Learning Objectives

1. Understand the history and applications of electronics.

2. Identify and describe common electronic components.

History and Applications

One of the most noteworthy aspects of electronics is how little the fundamental principles have changed over time. Systems have become smaller and faster, and new gadgets emerge daily, yet most devices in use today are based on concepts and designs developed decades ago.

In 1958, Jack Kilby of Texas Instruments developed the first integrated circuit (IC), a groundbreaking invention that revolutionized electronics. Today, modern ICs, like the Intel® Core™ i7 Extreme Edition Processor, contain billions of transistors within a small package. This rapid miniaturization demonstrates the industry's relentless drive for innovation, while the underlying principles remain unchanged.

Miniaturization faces four main limitations:

  • Semiconductor Material Quality
  • Network Design Techniques
  • Manufacturing and Processing Equipment
  • Innovative Spirit in the Industry

Evolution of Microprocessors

The transistor count in microprocessors has grown exponentially, following Moore's Law, which states that the number of transistors on a chip doubles approximately every two years. This trend has fueled advancements in computational power and energy efficiency.

Evolution of Electronics: From Bulky to Miniature

The evolution of electronics has been marked by rapid miniaturization, from room-sized computers like ENIAC to modern smartphones and wearables. This progress has been driven by advancements in semiconductor technology, integrated circuits, and innovative design techniques.

Common Electronic Components and Definitions

  1. Voltage (V): The electrical potential difference between two points in a circuit. Measured in volts (V).
  2. Current (I): The flow of electric charge through a conductor, measured in amperes (A).
  3. Resistance (R): The opposition to current flow in a conductor, measured in ohms (Ω).
  4. Capacitance (C): The ability to store electrical energy in an electric field, measured in farads (F).
  5. Inductance (L): The property to oppose changes in current, measured in henries (H).
  6. Power (P): The rate of electrical energy consumption, measured in watts (W). ( P = VxI)
  7. Frequency (f): The number of cycles per second, measured in hertz (Hz).
  8. Semiconductor: A material with electrical conductivity between a conductor and an insulator.
  9. Diode: A component that allows current to flow in one direction.
  10. Transistor: A device for amplifying or switching electronic signals.
  11. Integrated Circuit (IC): A set of circuits on a small chip.
  12. Oscillator: A circuit generating periodic waveforms.
  13. Amplifier: A device that increases signal amplitude.
  14. Analog Signal: A continuous signal varying over time.
  15. Digital Signal: A discrete binary signal (0s and 1s).
  16. Ohm's Law: ( V = IxR)
  17. Impedance (Z): Total opposition to AC, combining resistance and reactance.
  18. Reactance (X): Opposition to AC by capacitors and inductors.
  19. Rectifier: A device converting AC to DC.
  20. Switch: A device controlling current flow.
